History

River rafting evolved from ancient, necessary river transportation to a popular adventure sport. While Indigenous peoples used early vessels, modern rafting began in the 1840s with inflatable rafts, becoming a recreational activity in the 1940s and surging in popularity during the 1960s-70s with commercial trips and improved equipment.

  Key Historical Developments


Ancient & Early Navigation: Historically, indigenous peoples, such as those along the Snake River, used canoes and rafts for transport and fishing. Early explorers also used boats for navigation, often out of necessity rather than pleasure.
19th Century - The First Rafts: In 1842, Lieutenant John Fremont and Horace H. Day are credited with building the first contemporary inflatable raft made of rubber, intended for surveying the Platte River.
1940s - The Start of Commercial Rafting: The first official commercial whitewater rafting trip was led by Clyde Smith on the Snake River in 1940, although it was sporadic. Following WWII, surplus rubber rafts made the activity more accessible.
1960s-1970s - Recreational Boom: Commercial rafting matured in the 1960s. In 1972, rafting gained major recognition when it was included in the Munich Olympic Games.
Modern Era: Today, rafting is a highly specialized commercial sport with modern materials, safety standards, and various whitewater classes, spanning from leisure float trips to competitive racing.
